Executive Summary

This comprehensive longitudinal study examines how primary care clinicians' engagement with Medicaid evolved from 2016 to 2019, tracking 220,665 clinicians across 40 states. The analysis reveals significant volatility in Medicaid participation patterns, with important implications for healthcare access and policy.

Key Findings

Participation Volatility is Significant

Nearly 40% of clinicians experienced major changes in Medicaid participation, challenging assumptions about provider network stability.

Overall Participation Pattern Distribution

The study reveals that 60% of clinicians maintained stable enrollee volumes, while approximately 20% experienced substantial increases and 20% saw major decreases in their Medicaid patient loads over the three-year period.

Predictive Factors

Higher Baseline Volume = Greater Stability

Clinicians serving more than 100 Medicaid patients initially were significantly more likely to maintain consistent participation levels throughout the study period.

Community Health Centers Show Resilience

Affiliation with Community Health Centers was consistently associated with greater participation stability and higher odds of increases across all specialties.

Rural Areas Face Greater Challenges

Rural practice settings were linked to higher odds of major decreases in Medicaid participation, highlighting geographic disparities in access.

Methodology

Data Sources & Analysis
This study utilized longitudinal data from the Transformed Medicaid Statistical Information System Analytic Files (T-MSIS TAF) combined with National Plan and Provider Enumeration System (NPPES) data. The analysis tracked changes in the number of unique Medicaid enrollees served annually by individual clinicians, with major changes defined as increases or decreases exceeding 90% of baseline enrollee volume from 2016.
Statistical Approach
Multivariable logistic regression models were employed to identify characteristics associated with major increases or decreases in participation, controlling for clinician demographics, practice characteristics, and state-level policy factors. Models were stratified by specialty and profession to capture heterogeneity in practice patterns.

Policy Implications

  • Medicaid participation is more volatile than previously understood, requiring continuous monitoring and adaptive policies
  • Community Health Centers should be prioritized in managed care contracting strategies due to their demonstrated stability
  • Rural areas need targeted workforce retention programs to address higher participation volatility
  • Provider network adequacy standards should account for participation fluctuations beyond simple provider counts
  • Proposed Medicaid funding cuts could exacerbate existing participation instability, particularly affecting vulnerable populations
